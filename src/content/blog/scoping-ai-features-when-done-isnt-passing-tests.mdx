Most product managers I've worked with this year are doing one of two things wrong when they scope an AI feature.

Either they pretend the success criterion is the same as a regular software feature ("the API returns 200, and the output contains a string"), and then they're surprised when the team ships something that technically passes but is, in practice, useless. Or they go the other way &mdash; "AI is non-deterministic, you can't really test it" &mdash; and they ship without any real notion of done, which means the team is debating quality based on vibes for weeks after launch.

Neither of these is fine.

## What "done" actually looks like

For an AI feature, "done" is usually three things stacked:

1. **A behavioral specification.** Plain prose, written before the work starts. "The system should refuse to answer questions about competitor products." "The system should generate at most a three-sentence response unless explicitly asked for more." This is the part that often doesn't exist, and it's where everything else falls apart.

2. **A small but real eval set.** Not a massive benchmark &mdash; 30 to 100 cases that represent the actual surface area of the feature. Hand-built. Covering the obvious flow, the edge cases the team has already discussed, and a few cases that the team disagrees about (those are the most useful).

3. **A pass-rate target on that eval set.** Not 100%. Probably not 95% either, for non-trivial features. Pick a number, write down why, and treat it as a gating criterion for the rollout.

The eval set is the artifact that should be checked into the repo. Everything else flows from it.

## The trap

The trap, when scoping these features, is to defer the eval-set construction to "once we have a working system." This is backwards. The eval set is what defines the working system. If you don't know what cases the system needs to handle, you can't tell whether what you've built is the right thing or just *a* thing.

The pattern we've settled into: the PM writes the behavioral specification in week one. The PM and the engineering lead together write the first 30 eval cases in week one or two. Implementation begins after that. The eval set grows as the team encounters new categories of input.

## Why this is harder than it sounds

Writing eval cases is not natural work for most product teams. It feels like QA, and QA has been someone else's job for a long time. The cases also surface disagreements about what the product should do, which is uncomfortable but is exactly why they're useful.

The most successful teams I've worked with this year are the ones where the PM writes eval cases on Friday afternoons. It sounds trivial. It's the single highest-leverage thing they do.
