The cost structure of a small consultancy used to be straightforward: most of the work was implementation, most of the implementation was done by mid-level engineers, and the senior partners spent their time on scope definition, customer management, and the small slice of the engineering work that the mid-levels couldn't handle on their own.

Coding agents collapse the middle of that pyramid.

## The old shape

A typical six-week engagement at a small AI consultancy in 2022 looked like this:

- Two seniors splitting roughly 25% of their time on the project &mdash; scoping, architecture, the hard parts.
- Three to four mid-level engineers doing implementation full-time.
- Some fraction of a designer, a PM, and ops support shared across projects.

Total team size to do meaningful work: six to eight people. Total cost to the customer: high enough that engagements had to be at least six weeks to justify the ramp-up.

## The new shape

The same engagement today &mdash; we know, because we've now run it &mdash; looks like:

- Two seniors full-time.
- Coding agents doing the work that mid-level engineers used to do.
- No mid-levels, no PM, no designer-for-hire.

The seniors do the scoping, the architecture, *and* the implementation, because the implementation is mostly directing the agents and reviewing their output. The work that used to feel like "I'm overqualified for this" no longer exists &mdash; the agent does it.

## What this changes about scope

The interesting consequence isn't price. It's scope shape.

A two-person team with coding agents can take on engagements that used to be too small to be worth a six-person team. Four-week projects become viable. Highly focused projects &mdash; "build the one thing that does the one thing" &mdash; become first-class engagements rather than scope-stretches.

Conversely, very large engagements get harder. The two-person constraint is real. We won't take on something that requires sustained throughput from five people for three months &mdash; that's still a big-team problem, and coding agents don't fix it.

## What we tell prospective customers

Three rules:

1. **Scope is the conversation.** If you can describe what you want in a paragraph, we can probably do it. If it takes a deck, it's not the right shape for the studio.
2. **We don't grow the team.** Adding a third person changes the economics. We'd rather refer you to a bigger shop.
3. **The end of the engagement is real.** We hand off running infrastructure. No retainers, no indefinite "we're available if you need us."

The point isn't that consultancies should be small. It's that the shape of "consultancy" is now negotiable in a way it wasn't three years ago, and we're betting on the small shape because we think it produces better work for the engagements that are actually interesting.
